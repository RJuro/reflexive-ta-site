# Candidate themes

7 themes — claims with supporting and contradicting codes. Candidates for a human to refine.

## T01 — Migration unfolded not as a single decisive act but as a prolonged chain of contingent events — chance deaths, military conscription, bureaucratic gatekeeping — that determined who moved, when, and at what cost, with mothers disproportionately bearing the paperwork load and family members scattered across nations rather than traveling as a unit.

**Supporting:** Mother-daughter migrant pair; Father's emigration to the United States; Infant death of a sister; Theft of belongings in transit; Departure date as anchored memory; Trieste as first transit halt; Acquaintance-mediated rescue in transit; Ship named America; Learning about trip via father's letters and tickets; Mother's bureaucratic process for emigration papers; Last-minute intensification of approval process; Mandatory military service disrupting family emigration; Brother's accidental death reshaping migration plan; Chance and tragedy as shapers of family migration outcomes; Rail travel to Trieste embarkation; Discrepancy between paid and assigned ship class; Fellow passenger intercession enabling upgrade; Shipboard diet as Italian ship standard; Migrants with illness repatriated at sea; Health inspection and vaccination at US entry; Multi-leg rail transit via Chicago to Utah; Reunion with father and final bus leg to Sunnyside; Maternal birthplace in Warsaw; Family reconstitution through second marriage; Family dispersal across nations; Military conscription as emigration trigger; Staged family reunification through tickets; Family preservation through strategic male departure

**Contradicting / minority:** Uncertainty about transit details; Mother's paperwork burden relative to others; Speaker as active agent in the journey

## T02 — Fathers' prior departures and their progressive bodily decline gave migration its purpose (reunion) and its trajectory (relocation to follow work or escape conscription), making paternal absence the organizing pole around which maternal labor, child anticipation, and family decisions revolved.

**Supporting:** Father's emigration to the United States; Relocation to Colorado prompted by father's failing health; Enthusiasm for postwar emigration to the United States; Anxious anticipation focused on father reunion; Father as absent anchor of the migration project; Reunion with father and final bus leg to Sunnyside; Father's coal mining livelihood in Utah; Migration trajectory dictated by father's work site; Father's progressive health decline and work incapacity; Health-driven family relocation decision; Father's overseas absence; Marriage upon father's return; Military conscription as emigration trigger; Family preservation through strategic male departure; Retention of paternal memory despite early age; Mother's contentment tied to father's presence; Father's happiness in America

**Contradicting / minority:** Companion migrant group to same Utah town; Speaker as active agent in the journey; Emotional divergence from mother over leaving home

## T03 — Mothers performed the physical, agricultural, and bureaucratic labor — farming, slaughtering, chopping wood, securing emigration papers, raising children, fleeing in-law persecution — that sustained families through scarcity and displacement; this gendered labor was narrated as ordinary rather than remarkable, and infused migration decisions with relational and dignity-based priorities.

**Supporting:** Maternal subsistence farming and barter economy; Close proximity of animals to domestic living space; Peasant home as modest three-room dwelling; Mother performing manual wood processing alone; Normalization of female physical labor and hardship; Brother's departure leaving mother and child alone; Mother's bureaucratic process for emigration papers; Last-minute intensification of approval process; Gendered division of migration labor; Mother's restricted domestic role in mining town; Mother's given name; Mother-in-law's hostile blame; Flight to London from family conflict; Migration as escape from domestic persecution; Twin pregnancy as migration marker; Maternal caregiving anxiety; Mother prohibits children accepting money; Maternal enforcement of dignity over economic opportunism; Mother's contentment tied to father's presence; Mother's identity through marital bond

**Contradicting / minority:** Marriage and urban settlement in Denver; Female entry into paid labor during Depression; Occupational gender reversal through marriage

## T04 — Pre-existing kinship networks, ethnic clustering in mining and urban housing, and religious institutions (kosher observance, designated shipboard sections, neighborhood peer support) functioned as a practical and moral infrastructure that reduced migrant vulnerability and made new settlement possible — though kinship ties could also wound or fail.

**Supporting:** Extended kinship network upon arrival; Pre-existing transnational network; Company housing and ethnic clustering; Austrian settlers as local elite; Acquaintance-mediated rescue in transit; Mutual aid across migrant families; Companion migrant group to same Utah town; Peer-mediated language acquisition; Ethnic enclave as adaptive infrastructure; Relative material privilege; Placement in Jewish section on ship; Kosher food provision by ship chef; Continuity of kosher household practice; Preservation of Jewish identity during migration transit; Welcoming relatives in America; Father's bakery as family gathering hub; Kinship as anchor of immigrant experience

**Contradicting / minority:** Theft of belongings in transit; Vulnerability of women and children in transit; Mother-in-law's hostile blame

## T05 — Narrators situate their lives within successive dissolving polities — Austria-Hungary becoming Yugoslavia, partitioned Poland, shifting borders, conscripting armies of uncertain national affiliation — registering geopolitical transformation as lived background rather than personal political awakening, and embedding personal identity in now-vanished states.

**Supporting:** Post-WWI fragmented sea voyage; Geographic origin in shifting-border region; Geopolitical remapping as personal backdrop; Wartime disruption of family contact and remittances; Government-mandated hosting of war refugees; Cross-lingual accommodation with Italian refugees; Slovanian-German bilingualism; German as administrative lingua franca; Childhood memory of imperial collapse; Sarajevo assassination as WWI origin; Wartime commodity restrictions; Urban-rural wartime resource asymmetry; Personal identity anchored to dissolving polities; War trauma shaping reluctance to return; Family's wartime suffering in the homeland; Property transfer to homeland kin; Family dispersal across nations; Uncertainty over army and national identity

**Contradicting / minority:** Enthusiasm for postwar emigration to the United States; Resigned wartime compliance; Anxious anticipation focused on father reunion

## T06 — Rapid entry into American schools and immigrant women's entry into paid labor (meatpacking, baking) gave way to daughters' credentialed careers, marking a generational trajectory of class mobility that nonetheless coexisted with the obsolescence of homeland cultural capital (German, Slovakian literacy, traditional trades) and persistent hardship.

## T07 — Immigrant narratives are visibly constructed from fragments — maternal oral tradition, photographs, halting recollection — with emotionally resonant sites (family bakery, father's reunion, Lower East Side horses, named animals) preserved while iconic institutional sites (Ellis Island) vanish; an understated moral register ('the best we could,' 'all right,' 'nice time') simultaneously conveys resilience and forecloses on elaboration of suffering.
